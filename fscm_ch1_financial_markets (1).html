IL&FS Crisis (2018): When a Financial Market Fails its Economy

Infrastructure Leasing & Financial Services (IL&FS), a major NBFC, suddenly defaulted on short-term loans (Commercial Papers) worth ₹91,000 crore. This triggered a credit market freeze — mutual funds holding these CPs panicked, redemption pressure spiked, and NBFCs across India couldn't raise funds. The cascading effect slowed GDP growth and impacted automobile, real estate & shadow banking sectors.

📌 Exam Insight: This perfectly illustrates how Money Market (CP defaults) → Capital Market (MF panic) → Real Economy (credit freeze) linkages work — exactly what Section 2.2 (Linkages in Financial Flows) tests. The IL&FS crisis also showed why SEBI regulation of intermediaries and IRDAI oversight of insurance companies investing in these instruments matters enormously.

What is a Financial Market?

Basics
A Financial Market is a marketplace providing an avenue for the sale and purchase of financial assets such as equity stocks, bonds, foreign exchange, commodities, derivatives, etc. It channels funds from surplus units (savers) to deficit units (borrowers/investors).

Importance / Functions of Financial Markets

Section 1.2 & 2.1
Function What it means
📈 Productive Use of Savings Banks and FIs channel idle savings into loans for homes, education, business — savings alone add no value; investment does.
🎯 Price Discovery Once a security is listed, buy/sell trades reveal the market's valuation of a company's future prospects. Called Price Discovery.
💧 Liquidity Creation Buyers & sellers can trade anytime (given a counterpart). This makes assets liquid and incentivizes more investment.
💰 Lower Transaction Cost Exchanges and associations disseminate information freely — reducing the cost of getting data about securities.
🏗 Capital Access Individuals, companies, governments get access to capital they couldn't otherwise raise directly.
👷 Job Creation Brokers, underwriters, merchant bankers, custodians, depositories — entire industries emerge around financial markets.

  • Shareholders (own the company; buy low, sell high)

Key Entities: Deep Dive

Exam-Favourite
U Underwriters — How They Make Money

Underwriters guarantee subscription of unsubscribed shares during IPOs. They charge a small fee. Their profit = Difference between pre-IPO price paid and public offer price.

⚡ Exam Trap: Underwriters are service providers, NOT primary stakeholders. Companies are primary stakeholders, not underwriters.
D Depositories vs Custodians — Know the Difference

Depositories (NSDL, CDSL): Maintain investor accounts in electronic form; transfer ownership on trades; accessed via Depository Participants (DPs).

Custodians (SHCIL, SBI ShareHolding): Provide safe-keeping + collect dividends + transfer certificates + update client status. Fee: ~1% of transaction volume. Note: US funds require custodians with USD 200 million capital — no Indian custodian qualifies, so foreign banks (Citi, Deutsche, HSBC) serve FIIs.

🇮🇳 SEBI (India) — Est. 1992
  • Regulates securities exchanges
  • Registers & regulates intermediaries, MFs
  • Promotes & regulates SROs
  • Prohibits insider trading & fraud
  • Promotes investor education
  • Regulates takeovers
  • Limited penalty amount — weaker deterrent
🇺🇸 SEC (USA) — Global Counterpart
  • Same functions as SEBI broadly
  • Must be registered with SEC & join SRO
  • Can impose unlimited fine — stronger deterrent
  • More enforcement "teeth" than SEBI
  • Acts as effective deterrent vs malpractices
  • FDMs regulated by CFTC + NFA (for forex)
R RBI vs Fed (USA)

RBI (India): Primarily driven by inflation control (even at expense of growth). Keeps interest rates higher to discourage excess borrowing.

Federal Reserve (USA): Primarily driven by growth and employment (even at expense of inflation). Low interest rates encourage industry borrowing.

Money Market Instruments

Section 4.1
Treasury Bills (T-Bills) Govt

Short-term instruments issued by Central Government. Issued at discount, redeemed at par.

Maturity: <1 Year 91d / 182d / 364d Zero coupon
Cash Management Bills (CMBs) Govt

Issued by GoI to meet temporary cash flow mismatches. Similar to T-Bills but shorter.

Maturity: <91 days Discount to par
Call / Notice / Term Money Banks

Short-term borrowing/lending between banks & Primary Dealers.

Call: 1 day Notice: 2-14 days Term: 15d+
Certificate of Deposits (CDs) Banks

Issued by banks for short-term funding. Used when credit growth > deposit growth.

3, 6, 12 months Discount → Par Bulk issuance
Commercial Papers (CPs) Corporates

Issued by corporates (especially NBFCs), primary dealers. Higher risk & yield than T-Bills.

Discount to FV Higher yield NBFCs major issuer
Repurchase Agreements (Repo) RBI

Seller's view = Repo; Buyer's view = Reverse Repo. Short-term loans against Govt securities approved by RBI.

Collateral: G-Sec, T-Bills RBI approved
🔑 CDs vs CPs: Both are short-term instruments issued at discount. CDs are issued by Banks; CPs are issued by Corporates. CPs have higher risk & yield than T-Bills.

Practice MCQs

10 Questions
Question 1 · ICAI Official
The derivatives market deals in contracts whose value is based on the market value of the asset being traded, called the ___________.
A Forwards
B Futures
C Options
D Underlying
✅ Underlying — The asset whose value drives the derivative contract is called the "underlying." Forwards, futures, and options are all types of derivatives — not the underlying itself.
Question 2 · ICAI Official
Which among the following is NOT a function of Financial Markets?
A Puts savings into more productive use
B Determines the price of securities
C Makes financial assets risky and liquid
D Lowers the cost of transactions
✅ Option C — Financial markets make assets liquid, NOT risky. The word "risky" makes this incorrect. Markets actually help manage and reduce risk through diversification and derivatives.
Question 3 · ICAI Official
Which among the following is the PRIMARY stakeholder in the financial market?
A Merchant Bankers
B Brokers
C Underwriters
D Companies
✅ Companies — Primary stakeholders are: Shareholders, Lenders, Companies, and Mutual Fund Orgs. Merchant Bankers, Brokers, and Underwriters are Service Providers, not primary stakeholders.
Question 4 · ICAI Official
The ___________ make their profit on the difference in price between what they paid before the IPO and when the shares are offered to the public.
A Underwriters
B Merchant Bankers
C Brokers
D Custodians
✅ Underwriters — They buy shares before listing and profit from the spread between their cost and the public offer price. They also earn fees for guaranteeing subscription of unsubscribed portions.
Question 5 · ICAI Official
___________ is a voluntary market body for the bond, money, and derivatives markets.
A AMFI
B FIMMDA
C AIBI
D FEDAI
✅ FIMMDA — Fixed Income Money Market and Derivatives Association of India. AMFI = Mutual Funds. FEDAI = Forex. AIBI = Investment Bankers.
Question 6 · Concept-Based
Cash Management Bills (CMBs) are different from Treasury Bills primarily because:
A CMBs are issued by State Governments
B CMBs are issued at face value
C CMBs have maturity less than 91 days & meet temporary cash flow mismatches
D CMBs are issued by RBI directly
✅ Option C — CMBs are issued by GoI (not state govts or RBI) to specifically meet temporary cash flow mismatches. They mature in <91 days. Like T-Bills, they are issued at discount to par.
Question 7 · Application
SEBI differs from SEC (USA) in that:
A SEBI regulates mutual funds; SEC does not
B SEC can impose unlimited fines, while SEBI has limited penalty powers
C SEBI was established much earlier than SEC
D SEC does not prohibit insider trading
✅ Option B — The key difference is penalty power. SEC can impose unlimited fines, making it a stronger deterrent. SEBI has limited fine amounts. Both otherwise perform similar regulatory functions.
Question 8 · Tricky
Which of the following is correctly matched — Instrument : Issuer?
A CPs → Banks
B CDs → Corporates (NBFCs)
C T-Bills → Central Government
D CMBs → State Governments
✅ Option C — T-Bills are issued by the Central Government. CDs → Banks; CPs → Corporates/NBFCs; CMBs → Central Government (not state). This is a common trap question!
Question 9 · Conceptual
The Buffett Indicator measures:
A P/E ratio of the Sensex
B Total market capitalization of listed shares divided by GDP
C Foreign institutional investor holdings as % of market cap
D Ratio of bond market size to equity market size
✅ Option B — The Buffett Indicator = Market Cap / GDP. It shows the extent to which the market values listed corporates relative to the economy. A high ratio may indicate overvaluation.
Question 10 · Advanced
According to Sensex historical data, equity investments held for 15 years or more:
A Gave positive returns in about 90% of cases
B Gave positive returns in 27 out of 29 years
C Gave positive returns in ALL holding periods (27/27)
D Gave returns equal to bank FD rates
✅ Option C — For 15-year (27/27) and 20-year (22/22) holding periods, Sensex ALWAYS gave positive returns. For 10 years it was 28/29 = almost always. Only 1-year returns were truly uncertain (25/38).
