What is Credit Rating?
Core definition, objectives, and uses

Definition

A credit rating is a rating agency's opinion on the likelihood of default on principal and interest payments for a debt instrument. Expressed via a simple alphanumeric symbol — it is not a guarantee of performance.

❌ Not insurance against default ❌ Not a performance judgment ❌ Not for equity (usually) ❌ Not linked to legal compliance ✅ Dynamic & subject to change ✅ Instrument-specific
🎯
Objectives
  • Rate debt obligations of companies
  • Guide investors on repayment risk
  • Spread awareness among corporates & regulators
  • Create an environment for debt rating
  • Build investor confidence
  • Encourage quality-consciousness in issuers
👤
Uses for Investors
  • Aids investment decisions
  • Provides credible, standardised information
  • Saves time & cost of in-depth analysis
  • Enables comparison across instruments
🏢
Uses for Issuers
  • Meets SEBI regulatory obligations
  • Helps raise capital at lower interest rates
  • Enhances goodwill & market image
  • Serves as a marketing tool

Rating Services
Types of credit ratings offered — illustrated via CRISIL
🏭
(i) Corporate Sector Ratings

For companies launching debt instruments. CRISIL uses a three-pronged approach: Business RiskManagement RiskFinancial Risk along with parent/group support.

🏦
(ii) Financial Sector Ratings

For banks, NBFCs, housing finance, securities firms. Uses the CRAMEL framework: CapitalResource RaisingAsset QualityManagementEarningsLiquidity

🔗
(iii) Structured Finance

For complex instruments such as CDOs, synthetic instruments, and syndicated loans — where conventional financing is insufficient. Ratings capture nuances of underlying asset classes.

💳
(iv) Credit Quality Ratings

Expresses expected default probability of debt securities held by debt funds. Assigned only to funds investing entirely in debt.

🔄
(v) Recovery Risk Rating

Measures variability in post-default recovery. Key drivers: collateral coverage, asset quality, seniority of instrument.

📉
(vi) Expected Loss (EL) Ratings

Developed for operational infrastructure projects. Seven-point scale EL1–EL7, where EL1 = lowest expected loss. Considers both probability of default and post-default recoveries.

🛡️
(vii) Insurance Hybrids

For subordinated debt / preference shares of insurers (permitted by IRDA). Considers industry risk, business risk, financials, risk management, and parental support.

⚖️
(viii) Independent Credit Evaluation (ICE)

For resolution plans of large accounts (≥ ₹1 billion). Mandated by RBI (Aug 2020) for COVID-19 stress resolution plans.

🏗️
(ix) REITs / InvITs Ratings

For Real Estate & Infrastructure Investment Trusts. Focuses on underlying asset portfolio quality, cash flows, leverage, and investment manager experience.

Credit Rating Process
Step-by-step flow from issuer request to ongoing monitoring
Three Key Determinants of a Rating
Issuer's ability to pay Security owner's claim strength Economic significance of industry
a
Request & Analysis

Issuer approaches the agency. A team gathers info on historical performance, competitive position, business strategies, financial policies, and industry context.

b
Rating Committee

Analysts present their findings to an internal Rating Committee. The issuer does not participate — this is an independent evaluation.

c
Communication & Appeal

Rating is shared with the issuer along with supporting rationale. If the issuer disagrees, they may appeal with additional material information.

d
Public Announcement

If accepted by the issuer, the rating agency makes a public announcement. Unaccepted ratings may still be disclosed on the agency's website.

e
Monitoring

Ratings are kept under continuous watch throughout the instrument's tenure. Annual reviews are carried out when no change is needed.

f
Rating Watch

A rated instrument may be placed on "Rating Watch" if significant developments arise. This triggers a full-scale review that may upgrade or downgrade the rating.

g
Rating Coverage

Ratings cover public utilities, financial institutions, infrastructure, SPVs, MNCs (via guarantees/LoCs), and Corporate Governance Ratings.

Credit Rating Methodology
Four broad pillars analysed by credit rating agencies
⚠️
(i) Business Risk

Risk of lower profits than anticipated. Divided into four sub-categories:

🧭 Strategic Risk

When a company's strategy becomes less effective due to tech changes, new competitors, shifting demand. e.g. Kodak ignoring digital cameras; Nokia missing touch screens.

📋 Compliance Risk

Risk of failing to comply with laws in a new sector or geography. Especially relevant under Companies Act 2013 and evolving SEBI guidelines.

⚙️ Operational Risk

Internal failure in people or processes — e.g. erroneous payments. Locational advantage, labour relations, and cost structure can improve ratings.

🌐 Reputational Risk

Usually follows a business risk event. e.g. GM's India exit hurt its reputation. Conversely, adding new products boosts investor confidence.

💰
(ii) Financial Risk

Unexpected changes in financial conditions — prices, exchange rates, interest rates, credit ratings.

Counter Party Risk — Non-honouring of obligations (delivery failure, default on borrowings)


Political Risk — Adverse host-country government actions: confiscation, currency restrictions, patent invalidation, price controls


Interest Rate Risk — Changes in rates alter asset/liability values; critical for banks

Currency Risk — Cash flows affected by exchange rate movements. Exporters gain; importers lose when rupee depreciates


Accounting Risk — Deviations from standard accounting (overstatement, off-balance-sheet liabilities) distort credit analysis

👔
(iii) Management Evaluation

Assessed via the MD&A Report (mandatory for listed companies). Key factors: track record, managerial talent depth, succession plans, capacity to handle adversity, and management philosophy.

🌍
(iv) Business Environment Analysis

Examines external factors — new laws, consumer trends, technology shifts, macro-economic conditions (recession risk, inflation). Strategies are then developed to counter negative impacts.

CAMELS Model
International rating system used by bank supervisory authorities for financial institutions
C
Capital Adequacy
Assesses capital adequacy through capital trend analysis and compliance with risk-based net-worth regulations.
A
Asset Quality
Evaluates loan quality, fair market vs book value of investments, recoverability of receivables.
M
Management
Measures management involvement, decision-making quality, teamwork, and ability to respond to financial stress.
E
Earnings
Assesses the institution's ability to sustain, expand, and remain competitive through earnings growth and net margins.
L
Liquidity
Working capital management effectiveness and the corporate's ability to meet short-term commitments.
S
Sensitivity
Verifies vulnerability of the institution to specific risk exposures — internal and external factors.
💡
How CAMELS is applied

Rating agencies weigh the positive and negative aspects under each CAMELS category. The net score across all six dimensions feeds into the overall creditworthiness rating of the issuer and, by extension, the instrument.

🔄
Rating Revisions & Default Rates

SEBI mandates continuous monitoring throughout an instrument's lifetime. Key metrics:

Default Rate

% of rated firms that defaulted in a given period. Higher rating = lower default probability. CRISIL AAA: 0.00% over 3 years.

Transition Rate

% of firms whose ratings changed. Between FY11–21, 96.28% of CRISIL AA entities maintained their rating; 1.3% were upgraded.

Credit Rating Agencies
Key agencies in India and globally — all recognised by SEBI

🇮🇳 India

CRISIL
Credit Rating Information Services of India Ltd. (CRISIL)
Founded pre-reforms era. India's leading agency with 60%+ market share. Tied up with Standard & Poor's (USA). Covers 1,800+ companies and 3,600+ instruments.
ICRA
Investment Information and Credit Rating Agency (ICRA)
Began in 1991. Largest shareholder is Moody's Investor Services (via its Indian subsidiary). Covers 2,500+ instruments.
CARE
Credit Analysis and Research Ltd. (CARE)
Established in 1993. Promoted by UTI, IDBI, and Canara Bank. Covers 2,500+ instruments.
Fitch
Fitch Ratings India (P) Ltd.
100% subsidiary of the international Fitch Group. Covers corporates, governments, structured finance, and debt instruments.
ONICRA
Onida Individual Credit Rating Agency (ONICRA)
India's first individual credit rating company (1993). Provides ratings for individual loan applicants, employee screening, and SME ratings.

🌍 International

S&P
Standard & Poor's (S&P)
150+ years in credit rating. World's leading provider. Operates in ~28 countries.
Fitch
Fitch Ratings
Top-3 global agency. HQ in New York & London. Uses AAA–D scale (introduced 1924). Operations in 30+ countries.
M
Moody's Ratings
Global presence in 36 countries, ~10,700 employees. Helps build transparent, integrated financial markets. Revenue: $3.6 billion (2016).
🚨
Credit Rating Agencies & the US Sub-Prime Crisis (2007–08)

Agencies were criticised for assigning high ratings (AAA) to MBS and CDO instruments backed by subprime mortgages (≈ $3.2 trillion in risky loans). These instruments collapsed, leading to $523 billion in bank write-downs by September 2008. Conflict of interest was a key issue — complex securities earned agencies 3× more in fees than traditional corporate bonds.

Limitations of Credit Rating
Key shortcomings and governance concerns
1
Rating Changes

Ratings can change over time. Downgrades may not happen quickly enough to protect investors. Ratings do not guarantee financial strength.

2
Industry-Specific, Not Company-Specific

Agencies focus on macro trends and industry-level factors rather than individual company performance, potentially over-reacting to sector cycles.

3
Cost-Benefit Analysis Ignored

Mandatory rating requirements remove cost-benefit judgment. Rating should ideally be optional, allowing corporates to self-assess.

4
Conflict of Interest

Agencies are paid by the same entities they rate. In a competitive market, this creates pressure to assign favourable ratings.

5
Reliance on Borrower-Provided Information

Ratings are based on information supplied by the issuer, which may be inaccurate, incomplete, or selectively presented.

6
Lack of Accountability

Credit rating is merely an opinion — agencies are not legally accountable. Inexperienced staff may draw incorrect conclusions.

7
Corporate Governance Issues

Revenue concentration from a single client group, aggressive competitive practices, and insufficient disclosure of rating assumptions all undermine the system's integrity.

📡
Contemporary Developments
  • SEBI mandates disclosure of probability of default benchmarks for each rating category (1-year, 2-year, 3-year CDRs), aligning India with global standards.
  • RBI has encouraged rating agencies to use AI, machine learning, and social media monitoring to catch stress signals proactively before defaults occur.
  • Agencies may be granted access to RBI's CRILC (Central Repository of Information on Large Credits) database.
