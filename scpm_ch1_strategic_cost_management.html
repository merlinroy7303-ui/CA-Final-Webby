A. Managing Cost Strategically

"It is not sufficient to simply reduce costs; instead, costs must be managed strategically." — Cooper & Slagmulder (1998)
🔄 Evolution of Cost Concepts
Stage 1
Cost Ascertainment
Recording & ascertaining costs only (Cost Accounting)
Stage 2
Cost Control
Containment of cost — maintaining status quo
Stage 3
Traditional CM
Cost reduction — cutting always = profit (flawed)
Stage 4
Strategic CM
Aligning costs to business strategy (modern approach)
Key Distinction: Cost Accounting = ascertainment. Management Accounting = control & management. SCM = strategic alignment.
⚠️

Limitations of Traditional Cost Management

🚫 Ignores Competition

Only looks at internal quantitative factors; ignores market growth and customer requirements.

🚫 Inferior Quality Risk

Excessive focus on cost reduction. Broad cuts lead to inferior quality products/services.

🚫 Static Data

Relies on historical financial accounting data — ignores dynamic marketing & economics.

🚫 Quantitative Only

Reviews only quantitative variances; misses qualitative deviations and strategic signals.

🚫 Reactive Approach

Corrective rather than preventive — responds after problems occur, not before.

🚫 Short-Term Focus

Focuses on next year/quarter/month. Lacks long-term strategic perspective.

⚖️

The Three Pillars of SCM

🔗

Value Chain Analysis

Sequential chain of activities delivering value to the customer. Identifies value-added vs non-value-added activities. Helps achieve cost leadership and differentiation.

🎯

Strategic Positioning Analysis

Analysis of company's relative position within its strategic segment. Studies culture, stakeholders, capabilities, and environment. Leads to differentiation or cost leadership.

⚙️

Cost Driver Analysis

Examination, quantification & explanation of monetary effects of cost drivers. Identifies structural and executional cost drivers to understand what truly drives costs.

Inter-relationship: Understanding the value chain → defines optimal strategic position → both together identify relevant cost drivers.

📦 Concept Insight — Value Shop Model

Value Shop Model (Alternative for Service Firms)

Conceptualised by James D. Thompson (1967), named by Stabell & Fjeldstad (1998). For service firms, the value chain analogue is the Value Shop — a problem resolution model rather than a value-creation model.

Primary Activities: Problem Finding & Acquisition → Problem Solving → Choice → Execution → Control → Evaluation (cyclic, not sequential)

Examples: BCG, McKinsey, Deloitte, BFSI sector, telecom services.

Value created by: More information than client, specialised analysis methods, strong expert professionals.
🎯

Strategic Positioning Analysis

Strategic Positioning Analysis includes study of 4 dimensions:
I. Culture, Beliefs & Assumptions
→ Vision and Values
Vision = aspiration statement; Values = guiding principles
II. Stakeholders' Influence & Expectations
→ Mission and Objectives
Use Mendelow's Matrix: High Power + High Interest = Key Players
III. Task Environment (Capabilities)
→ Strengths and Weaknesses
Resources and core competences; microenvironment analysis
IV. Macro Environment
→ Opportunities and Threats
Competitors, markets, regulations, PESTEL/STEEPLE factors
🔲 Mendelow's Matrix — Stakeholder Classification
HIGH POWER + HIGH INTEREST → Manage Closely / Key Players
Most important group. Actively engage; keep them updated and empowered to steer direction.
HIGH POWER + LOW INTEREST → Keep Satisfied
Potentially influential. Keep satisfied so they don't gain interest and exercise power.
LOW POWER + HIGH INTEREST → Keep Informed
Little individual power but could join forces with powerful groups. Keep them informed.
LOW POWER + LOW INTEREST → Monitor
Marginal players; unlikely to resist. Minimum effort required.
Tools for Strategic Position Analysis:
External: SWOT, PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, Porter's Diamond
Internal: Value Chain, CSFs & Core Competencies, Product Lifecycle, McKinsey 7S
⚙️

Cost Driver Analysis

🏗️ Structural Cost Drivers
Strategic choices about economic structure. Scale, scope, experience, technology, complexity.

Note: More is NOT necessarily better for structural drivers.
Examples: plant construction (scale), complexity (# product lines), process selection (technology used)
⚡ Executional Cost Drivers
Execution of business activities. Employee involvement, TQM, capacity utilization, plant efficiency, product configuration, supplier linkages.

Note: Higher is better for executional drivers.
Examples: employee utilization (degree of involvement), quality service provision (TQM approach)

B. Gaining Competitive Advantage

🌟 Differentiation Strategy
Deliver distinctive value to command a premium price. Example: Apple.
Sources: Quality (design, performance), Innovation, Customer relations, After-sales, Wide product range

Benefits: Huge margins at premium price OR build market share at lower-than-premium price
💰 Cost Leadership Strategy
Become the lowest cost producer in the industry while maintaining acceptable quality.
Sources: Cost-effective inputs, process innovation/re-engineering, low-cost distribution, learning curve, economies of scale

Ways: Reducing cost of individual value chain activities or Reconfiguring the value chain
⚠️ Porter's "Kiss of Death": Companies that try to be all things to all customers can get "stuck in the middle" — competing on neither cost nor differentiation effectively. Avoid this strategic mistake.

Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas

Nine-element canvas connecting 4 cost elements (left) through Value Proposition (centre) to 4 revenue elements (right). Proposed by Alexander Osterwalder (May 2013, HBR).
8. Key Partners
Who are our partners/key suppliers? What resources/activities do they provide?
6. Key Activities
Uniquely strategic things the business does to deliver its proposition
2. Value Proposition
What's compelling about the proposition? Why do customers buy/use? Solves problems or satisfies needs.
4. Customer Relationship
How do we get, keep & grow customers? Push vs Pull mode.
1. Customer Segments
Who are the customers? What do they think? See? Feel? Do?
7. Key Resources
Unique strategic assets (man, material, machine, method, money)
3. Channels
Physical or digital channels to deliver products/services
9. Cost Structure
Major cost drivers? Most expensive resources? Linked to revenue streams how?
5. Revenue Streams
Direct sale, transaction-based, freemium, license, subscription. How does business earn from its value proposition?
Elements 1, 2, 3, 4 = Customer focus | Elements 6, 7, 8 = Infrastructure | Elements 5, 9 = Financial viability
📜 Evolution of Business Model Concept
Margretta: 2 components — Producing something + Selling something
Johnson, Christensen & Kaggerman (2008): 3 components — Customer value proposition + Profit formula + Key resources & processes
Osterwalder (May 2013): 9-element Business Model Canvas — the standard today
🎨

Value Proposition Canvas

The VPC is a plugin for the Business Model Canvas — zooms into the Customer Segment and Value Proposition elements. Helps design, test, build and manage great customer value propositions.
💚 Value Map (Business Side)
Products & Services

Bundle of products/services offered to help customer get a functional, social or emotional job done

Pain Relievers

How products/services eliminate or reduce customer pains before, during, after job completion

Gain Creators

How products/services create customer gains — benefits and outcomes they desire

🟡 Customer Profile (Customer Side)
Customer Jobs

Important issues customers try to solve — functional, social, or emotional/personal jobs

Pains

Anything that annoys the customer before/during/after getting a job done — costs, risks, negative emotions

Gains

Outcomes/benefits customers require, expect, or desire — functional utilities, social gains, positive emotions, cost savings

Problem-Solution Fit: Features of value proposition map perfectly match the customer segment profile.
Product-Market Fit: When the market validates this match and the value proposition gets traction with real customers.

C. External Environment — Porter's Five Forces

Framework evolution: PEST → PESTEL (+ Environmental + Legal) → STEEPLE (+ Ethical). Industry analysis using Porter's Five Forces assesses intensity of competition and profitability potential. Higher intensity = lower profitability.
Threat of New Entrants
Depends on barriers to entry: economies of scale, capital, brand loyalty, gov't policy, switching costs
Bargaining Power of Suppliers
High when few suppliers, unique products, no substitutes, high switching costs
Industry Rivalry
Action-reaction dynamic. Stiffer when: more firms, homogeneous products, high fixed costs, high exit barriers
Bargaining Power of Buyers
High when: few buyers, large volumes, standardised products, low switching costs, full information
Threat of Substitutes
High when substitute is perfect and cheaper. Can be from different segments or industries
🔲 Barriers & Profitability Matrix
High Entry + Low Exit
High, Stable Returns
Best scenario
High Entry + High Exit
High, Risky Returns
High reward, high risk
Low Entry + Low Exit
Low, Stable Returns
Predictable but low
Low Entry + High Exit
Low, Risky Returns
Worst scenario

Understanding Customers & Market Segmentation

Criteria for a valid Segment: Homogeneous internally + Heterogeneous externally + Clearly identifiable + Reasonable size + Responsive to marketing offerings
📦

Product Segmentation

Based on products/features. E.g., Compact/Sedan/SUV; Fuel vs Electric vehicles

👥

Demographic Segmentation

Age, Gender, Family situation, Education, Ethnicity. E.g., Kids/Men/Women apparel lines

🧠

Psychographic Segmentation

Personality, values, lifestyle, beliefs. E.g., Economy/Business/First class airline seats

🛒

Behavioural Segmentation

Purchasing & spending habits, brand interactions. E.g., Flipkart Plus members

📍

Geographic Segmentation

ZIP code, city, country, climate, urban/rural. E.g., Regional product variations

⚔️ Basis of Competition
The reason why customers choose a particular business over its competitor. Can be product, feature, function, style, or availability.

Key questions:
• What drives demand in the industry?
• How does the customer choose (price, feature, quality)?
• How is price determined in the industry?
• What are the main cost drivers?
• What are current & potential risks?
🔑 Industry Key Success Factors (KSFs/CSFs)
Factors critical to success in generating and sustaining competitive advantage. Articulates what the firm MUST do well.

KPIs are attached to each CSF to measure performance.

CSFs are part of strategic planning (with strategic goals and strategic scope). Under dynamic environments, CSFs change over time — firms must periodically re-assess.
💎 Core Competencies Analysis (Prahalad & Hamel, 1990)
Core Competences = Things a company can do uniquely well that no one else can copy quickly enough to affect competition.
Relevance
Competence must strongly influence customers to choose your product
Difficulty of Imitation
Hard to copy, allowing sustained competitive position
Breadth of Application
Opens up a good number of potential markets (not just niche)

D. Information Technology — The Strategic Context

Data is the new oil. IT + IS + IM — three interconnected but distinct concepts. Together they have the potential to transform business, counter Porter's Five Forces, and revamp the value chain.
📊 IT vs IS vs IM Strategy — Earl's Framework (1989)
BasisIS StrategyIT StrategyIM Strategy
Resolve (Scope)WhatHowWhere
Driven ForceBusiness DrivenTechnology FocusedManagement Driven
DirectionalTop-DownBottom-upMulti-directional
OrientationDemand OrientedSupply OrientedRelationship Oriented
Organisational LevelDivision/SBU/FunctionActivity basedOrganisation wide
🔥 IT/IS Impact on Porter's Five Forces
Threat of New Entrants:
Existing players: Sophisticated IT creates expensive-to-replicate barriers
New entrants: Use IT to break down barriers (e.g., internet banking replacing branch networks)
Supplier Bargaining Power:
IT enables e-procurement, price comparison databases, supply chain integration with ERP — reduces supplier power
Buyer Bargaining Power:
Customer Data Warehousing & Mining for targeted marketing; IT-based loyalty programmes to "lock in" customers
Threat of Substitutes:
IT itself is a substitute (Kindle → books, laser printers → dot-matrix). Counter via patents, CAD/CAM for faster product development
Competitive Rivalry: Use JIT, ERP, MRP for cost leadership; collaborative ventures; IT for customer relationship building to reduce rival threats
⛓️ IT/IS Impact on the Value Chain
Inbound Logistics: Barcode systems, virtual warehouses, RFID tracking, MRP/ERP, JIT stock control
Operations: CAD/CAM, CIM, robots, automated guided vehicles, Edu-tech platforms, ATMs
Outbound Logistics: Vehicle scheduling, automated warehousing, RFID (UPS case study)
Marketing & Sales: CRM, digital/social/viral marketing, customer segmentation databases (Nike analytics case)
After-Sales Service: IoT-enabled remote service, ERP-integrated support, scheduling systems
🔍 Real-World Case Study — Nokia's Fall & the Lesson of Changing KSFs

Nokia: When Critical Success Factors Changed

Nokia was once the global market leader in mobile phones when KSFs were reliability and efficient distribution networks. When smartphones launched, the KSFs shifted to R&D capability, screen size, camera megapixels, app ecosystem, and internet connectivity.

Lesson: Nokia failed to recognise and adapt to changing CSFs. Under a dynamic business environment, CSFs may change — firms must periodically re-assess them. The same remote factor (technology shift) brought opportunity to emerging brands while threatening the incumbent market leader.

E. Management Accountant as a Leader

"If accounting is truly a language of business, then management accountant is indeed a poet, who appreciates value and numbers."
📊 Transformation of the Management Accountant's Role
Traditional Roles
1. Stewardship Accounting
2. Long-term & Short-term Planning
3. Developing MIS
4. Maintaining Optimum Capital Structure
5. Participating in Management Process
6. Providing Ease in Control
Modern / Extended Roles
7. Analysis — New insights from financial & non-financial data
8. Planning — Building informed long-term strategies
9. Innovation — Technology advancement & Industry 4.0 / 5.0
10. Leadership — From planning to execution
📢

Communication

Two-way process. Vertical/horizontal/diagonal. Communicating strategy, vision & values reduces resistance. MBWA technique — randomly visit workplace to engage employees informally.

🧩

Decision Making

Rational criteria: objective, fully informed, conscious, explicit, deliberate, consistent, logical, goal-oriented. Part of strategy development at every step.

⚖️

Business Ethics

Socio-economic approach: balance economic motives and social costs. Business orientation shifted from shareholder to stakeholder. Ethics must reflect organisational values.

👑 Leader vs. Manager — Key Distinctions
Leaders create vision; managers create goals
Leaders are change agents; managers maintain status quo
Leaders are unique; managers copy
Leaders take risks; managers control risk
Leaders are in it for long haul; managers think short-term
Leaders grow personally; managers rely on proven skills
Leaders build relationships; managers build systems
Leaders coach; managers direct
💡 Practical Insight — MBWA

Management by Walking/Wandering Around (MBWA)

Managers wander randomly through workplace to check on employees, equipment, and work status. Random sampling of events and discussions improves morale, productivity, and TQM.

Origin: Traced to executives at Hewlett-Packard (1970s). Similar to Japan's Gemba Walk method developed at Toyota.
Referenced by: Tom Peters & Robert H. Waterman in "In Search of Excellence" (1982).

Test Your Knowledge — MCQs

MCQ 01
Which of the following is NOT a pillar of Strategic Cost Management?
AValue Chain Analysis
BStrategic Positioning Analysis
CActivity Based Budgeting
DCost Driver Analysis
✅ Correct Answer: C
The three pillars of SCM are Value Chain Analysis, Strategic Positioning Analysis, and Cost Driver Analysis. Activity Based Budgeting is not a pillar of SCM (though ABC is a tool of SCM).
MCQ 02
Strategic Cost Management is the application of cost management techniques so that they ___ the strategic position of a firm and reduce costs.
AAlternatively improve
BSimultaneously improve
CTemporarily improve
DSequentially improve
✅ Correct Answer: B
The exact ICAI definition: "SCM is the application of cost management techniques so that they simultaneously improve the strategic position of a firm and reduce costs." This word is critical for exams.
MCQ 03
Inbound logistics in Porter's Value Chain covers which of the following?
APurchasing raw materials from vendors
BReceiving, storing, and handling raw material inputs
CNegotiating prices with suppliers
DFinding new external vendors
✅ Correct Answer: B
Inbound logistics = receiving, storing, and handling raw material inputs. It does NOT cover purchasing/procurement (options A, C, D fall under Procurement which is a support activity). This is a classic trap question!
MCQ 04
Business is said to achieve a 'product-market fit' when:
AFeatures of value proposition map match customer segment profile
BBusiness launches a new product for an existing market
CThe market validates the match and value proposition gets traction with real customers
DCost of production equals customer willingness to pay
✅ Correct Answer: C
Option A describes "Problem-Solution Fit" (features of VP map matching customer profile). Option C describes "Product-Market Fit" — when the market validates this match and VP gets traction with real customers.
MCQ 05
The Information Management (IM) strategy is characterised by which of the following?
ABusiness driven, Top-Down, Demand oriented
BTechnology focused, Bottom-up, Supply oriented
CManagement driven, Multi-directional, Relationship oriented
DBusiness driven, Bottom-up, Relationship oriented
✅ Correct Answer: C
Per Earl's Framework: IS = Business Driven/Top-Down/Demand Oriented. IT = Technology Focused/Bottom-up/Supply Oriented. IM = Management Driven/Multi-directional/Relationship Oriented. Organisation wide level.
MCQ 06
Which strategy does Michael Porter describe as the 'kiss of death'?
APure cost leadership with no differentiation
BFocusing only on niche markets
CTrying to be all things to all customers — getting stuck in the middle
DAggressive pricing below market rate
✅ Correct Answer: C
Porter called trying to compete on both cost AND differentiation simultaneously — without being excellent at either — the "kiss of death." Companies end up stuck in the middle, competitive on neither front.
MCQ 07
The Osterwalder Business Model Canvas has how many elements?
A7
B8
C9
D11
✅ Correct Answer: C
Osterwalder proposed a nine-element Business Model Canvas: 4 cost elements (Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources, Cost Structure) + 4 revenue elements (Customer Relationship, Channels, Customer Segments, Revenue Streams) + 1 central element (Value Proposition).
MCQ 08
RFID technology in value chain primarily benefits which primary activities?
AMarketing & Sales and After-Sales Service
BInbound Logistics and Outbound Logistics
COperations and After-Sales Service
DInbound Logistics and Operations only
✅ Correct Answer: B
RFID (Radio-Frequency Identification) uses electromagnetic fields to automatically identify and track tags attached to objects. It is used throughout the supply chain — primarily benefiting Inbound Logistics (tracking incoming materials) and Outbound Logistics (tracking finished goods deliveries).
MCQ 09
The Value Shop Model was conceptualised in 1967 by whom?
AStabell & Fjeldstad
BMichael E. Porter
CJames D. Thompson
DAlexander Osterwalder
✅ Correct Answer: C
The Value Shop Model was conceptualised by James D. Thompson in 1967. It was later named and defined by Stabell & Fjeldstad in 1998. Porter introduced the generic Value Chain (1985). Osterwalder introduced BMC (2013).
MCQ 10
For an effective market segment, which is NOT a required criterion?
AHomogeneous internally
BHeterogeneous externally
CGeographically concentrated
DResponsive to marketing offerings
✅ Correct Answer: C
The 5 criteria for a valid segment are: Homogeneous internally, Heterogeneous externally, Clearly identifiable, Reasonable/sufficient size, and Responsive. Geographic concentration is NOT required — segment basis can be demographic, psychographic, behavioural, etc.

Rapid Revision — 18 Key Points

1
SCM Definition: Application of cost management techniques to simultaneously improve strategic position and reduce costs.
2
3 Pillars: Value Chain Analysis + Strategic Positioning Analysis + Cost Driver Analysis (VSC).
3
Traditional CM vs SCM: Reactive vs Proactive | Short-term vs Long-term | Internal only vs Internal + External | Volume-based vs Activity-based costing.
4
Value Chain Primary: Inbound Logistics → Operations → Outbound Logistics → Marketing & Sales → After-Sales Service.
5
Value Chain Support: Firm Infrastructure, HR Management, Technology Development, Procurement. Increasing any support activity benefits at least one primary activity.
6
Margin: Value customer willing to pay minus cost incurred by firm. Goal of value chain is to maximise margin.
7
Competitive Advantage: Either Differentiation (premium price) or Cost Leadership (lowest cost). Porter's Kiss of Death = getting stuck in the middle.
8
BMC: 9-element canvas (Osterwalder, 2013). Value Proposition at centre connecting 4 cost elements (left) to 4 revenue elements (right).
9
VPC: Customer Profile (Jobs, Pains, Gains) + Value Map (Products/Services, Pain Relievers, Gain Creators). Fit = Problem-Solution Fit → Product-Market Fit.
10
Porter's Five Forces: Buyers' power, Suppliers' power, Threat of substitutes, Threat of new entrants, Rivalry among existing firms (BRASS mnemonic).
11
Barriers Matrix: High Entry + Low Exit = High, Stable Returns (best). High Entry + High Exit = High, Risky Returns.
12
Segmentation Bases: Product, Demographic, Psychographic, Behavioural, Geographic. Criteria: Homogeneous, Heterogeneous, Identifiable, Reasonable size, Responsive.
13
CSFs & KPIs: CSFs are critical success factors unique to each industry. KPIs are attached to each CSF to measure performance. CSFs change over time — reassess periodically.
14
IT vs IS vs IM: IT (How) = activity level, supply oriented, bottom-up. IS (What) = SBU level, demand oriented, top-down. IM (Where) = org-wide, relationship oriented, multi-directional.
15
RFID & Value Chain: Primarily benefits Inbound + Outbound logistics. RFID = radio-frequency identification for tracking items in supply chain.
16
Management Accountant Evolution: Traditional 6 roles expanded to 10 with Analysis, Planning, Innovation & Leadership. Now at crossroads of Technology, Financial Analysis, Strategic Planning & Leadership.
17
MBWA: Management by Walking Around — informal random workplace visits to improve morale, TQM, productivity. Origin: HP (1970s), similar to Toyota's Gemba Walk.
18
Value Shop Model: Alternative to Value Chain for service firms. Conceptualised by Thompson (1967), named by Stabell & Fjeldstad (1998). Focus = problem resolution, not value creation.
